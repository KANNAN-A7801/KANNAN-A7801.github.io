Library Book Detector using RFID

The Library Book Tracker project is designed to revolutionize library management through the implementation of an advanced system that combines RFID card technology, Arduino microcontrollers, and infrared (IR) sensors. In this innovative approach, each book is intricately linked to a unique RFID card. The system relies on IR sensors strategically placed throughout the library to enable real-time monitoring of book availability. The operational workflow involves library users presenting the RFID card associated with a particular book to the system. The IR sensors, integrated with Arduino technology, swiftly respond by detecting the presence or absence of the corresponding book in its designated location. This intricate process is repeated for each book in the library, providing an efficient and automated means of tracking and managing the availability of library resources. By seamlessly integrating RFID, Arduino, and IR sensor technologies, the Library Book Tracker not only enhances the overall efficiency of library operations but also offers a user-friendly experience for patrons seeking to locate specific books. This project represents a technological leap forward in library management, promising to streamline processes and improve accessibility to library resources.

Skin Cancer Detection using Deep Learning Techniques

Skin cancer is one of the most prevalent types of cancer worldwide, with early detection being crucial for successful treatment. In recent years, deep learning techniques, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have shown promising results in automating skin cancer detection from dermoscopic images. This study presents a comparative analysis of two popular CNN architectures, VGG and ResNet, for the task of skin cancer detection. The dataset used in this study consists of dermoscopic images of benign and malignant skin lesions. Preprocessing techniques including image normalization and augmentation are applied to enhance the dataset. Both VGG and ResNet architectures are trained on the dataset using transfer learning, leveraging pre-trained weights from ImageNet.
